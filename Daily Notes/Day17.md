# Topics Learned Today
 
## Topic 1: Fair Value Gaps (FVG)
 
A Fair Value Gap (FVG) is a visual representation of a market imbalance or inefficiency. These gaps occur when aggressive buying or selling creates a three-candle sequence where the first and third candles' wicks do not meet, leaving a hole in price action.
 
Traders use these zones as magnets, anticipating that the market will eventually return to fill the gap and seek equilibrium before continuing its original trend. While FVGs offer precise entry points and targets, they should **never be traded in isolation** — they must always be combined with market structure, trend direction, and other technical confluences to ensure a high-probability setup.
 
### How an FVG is Formed
 
An FVG is created when price moves up or down an "insane amount" over a very short period, typically forming a humongous candle. Because the move happens so fast, it does not give the opposing side — for example, sellers during a bullish surge — enough time to counteract the movement, leaving a gap behind.
 
### How to Mark an FVG on a Chart
 
Highlight the zone from:
- The **top wick** of the candle right before the big move, to
- The **lower wick** of the candle immediately after the big move.
Because the market naturally wants to fill these imbalances, price will often retrace back into this zone to retest it before continuing in the direction of the original massive move.
 
---
 
## Topic 2: Market Imbalance
 
A market imbalance occurs when the price of an asset moves up or down an insane amount in a very short period of time, typically forming a massive candlestick on the chart.
 
This extreme and rapid movement happens so quickly that the opposing side of the market doesn't get enough time to react or counteract the move. For example, during a massive bullish surge, price shoots up before sellers get the chance to step in, resulting in one-sided price movement. This specific lack of two-sided trading action is exactly what creates the imbalance.
 
This market imbalance is the underlying phenomenon that visually creates a Fair Value Gap on a price chart.
 
### Why Price Returns to Fill the Gap
 
Because the market naturally seeks equilibrium, opposing market participants eventually want to retest this unbalanced area. As a result, it is highly common for price to retrace back into the gap to fill the imbalance. Once filled, price will often resume its original massive trajectory to test previous highs or lows. Traders use this natural market behavior by waiting for the imbalance to be retested before entering a trade to profit from trend continuation.

## Topic 3: Break of Structure (As the Final FVG Validation Step)
 
A Break of Structure (BOS) serves as the sixth and final step in validating a Fair Value Gap. Simply put, a BOS happens when price successfully breaks past a high or low that it had previously established.
 
For an FVG to be considered viable and highly probable, the gap must be created **during or immediately after** this structural break. If an FVG forms but price fails to break the prior structural point, the gap is invalid and should not be traded.
 
### BOS by Market Direction
 
- **Bullish BOS:** First identify a previously established high to act as the "break of structure point." Wait to confirm price actually breaks past this high. Often, the same aggressive one-sided move that breaks this high is the exact move that generates the bullish FVG.
- **Bearish BOS:** Price must break past a previously established low. The bearish gap is only valid if this prior low is broken before or during the gap's creation.
Waiting for a BOS confirms that market momentum is strong enough to push past previous barriers. Combined with the other five validation factors, this helps filter out weak setups and identify the "ultimate fair value gap."

## Topic 4: Technical Analysis Tools for Filtering FVGs
 
### 1. The Gann Box Tool
 
A drawing tool configured to price levels of **0, 0.5, and 1**, which divides a selected price move exactly in half.
 
| Setup | How to Draw | Where to Trade |
|---|---|---|
| **Bullish** | Draw from absolute low to absolute high | Only target gaps in the **lower** half — ignore gaps in the upper half |
| **Bearish** | Draw from high to low | Only target gaps in the **upper** half |
 
This ensures you're only trading high-priority gaps within the move.
 
### 2. Support and Resistance Mapping
 
Adding confluence with historical support and resistance significantly strengthens an FVG. If a previously established support level aligns right in the middle of an untested bullish FVG, it creates a highly favorable confluence. The same principle applies when prior resistance coincides with a bearish FVG.
 
### 3. Candlestick Anatomy
 
- **Drawing the gap:** Must be drawn specifically from the top wick of the candle right before the big move to the lower wick of the candle immediately after it.
- **Confirming the entry:** When price retraces to test the gap, the reacting candle's wick can pierce entirely through the gap, but the **body** must close inside the gap or in the direction of the zone for a safe entry.
### 4. Market Structure Points
 
Traders must visually track and mark previous highs and lows to establish "break of structure points." Mapping these levels confirms whether the aggressive move successfully broke past a previous high (bullish) or previous low (bearish). An FVG is only valid if created during or after price breaks these structural points.

## Topic 5: The Six Key Factors for a Valid FVG
 
To ensure an FVG is valid and highly likely to succeed, all six of these factors must be true:
 
### 1. Must Be Unmitigated
The gap relies on price surging too quickly for the opposing side to react. It must remain completely **untested**. If price has already returned to test the zone, the gap is mitigated and invalid for future trades.
 
### 2. Proper Candle Reaction
When price returns to retest the gap, the reacting candle's **body** must close inside the FVG or in the direction of the zone. A wick can pierce entirely through the gap — that's fine — but if the body closes completely outside the gap, the setup is invalid.
 
### 3. Confluence with Support and Resistance
Finding a prior support level aligning in the middle of a bullish FVG (or resistance for a bearish FVG) creates a much stronger, higher-probability setup.
 
### 4. Positional Priority
When multiple FVGs form during a single trend, they are not equal:
 
| Trend | Highest Priority Gap |
|---|---|
| **Bullish** | The **lowest** gap on the chart (highest gap = weakest) |
| **Bearish** | The **highest** gap on the chart |
 
### 5. Utilizing the Gann Box Tool
As detailed above — only trade gaps in the correct half (lower half for bullish, upper half for bearish) of the Gann box drawn across the move.
 
### 6. Break of Structure (BOS)
The gap must occur during or immediately after price breaks a previously established high (bullish) or low (bearish). If the FVG forms without breaking that structural point, ignore it.
 
---
 
## Executing the "Ultimate Fair Value Gap" Trade
 
When a gap meets **all six criteria**, it becomes an "ultimate fair value gap" — with a greatly increased chance of success.
 
**Execution steps:**
1. Wait for price to drop into the unmitigated zone.
2. Verify the reacting candle closes safely inside or in the direction of the zone.
3. Once confirmed, enter the trade.
4. Set **stop loss** at the low of the specific candle that generated the FVG.
5. Set **take profit** at the high of the overall move.

# Questions I Had and Answers I Got
 
## Q1. What's the difference between a Fair Value Gap and an Order Block?
 
A Fair Value Gap is a price imbalance — a literal gap in price action caused by a rapid, one-sided move where wicks of the surrounding candles don't overlap. An Order Block, on the other hand, is the last opposing candle before that strong directional move — representing the zone where institutional orders were likely placed. They often occur together: the same aggressive move that creates an order block frequently also leaves behind an FVG right after it. Both are used as reaction zones, but an FVG is about the literal price hole, while an order block is about the candle that originated the move.
 
## Q2. Why does a wick piercing through the FVG not invalidate the setup, but a body closing outside it does?
 
A wick represents the highest or lowest price reached during that candle's timeframe, but it doesn't represent where buyers and sellers ultimately agreed on value — that's what the close (body) represents. If a wick pierces through the FVG and then the body closes back inside or in the direction of the zone, it shows that despite a brief overreaction, the zone was still respected and defended. However, if the body closes completely outside the gap, it shows that sellers (in a bullish FVG) or buyers (in a bearish FVG) have actually taken control beyond that zone, meaning the gap has failed to hold and the setup should be considered invalid.
 
## Q3. Why does positional priority favor the lowest bullish gap and the highest bearish gap?
 
This ties back to the concept of extreme zones learned on Day 15. The lowest gap in a bullish move sits closest to the origin of that move — similar to an extreme demand zone. Since it's closest to where the original momentum began, it represents the strongest area of unmitigated institutional interest. The same logic applies inversely for bearish gaps — the highest gap sits closest to the origin of the bearish move and represents the strongest area of selling interest. Gaps further away from the origin point are considered weaker because more price action and potential mitigation may have already occurred near them.
 
## Q4. Can an FVG be valid without a Break of Structure?
 
No. As covered in the six key factors, a Break of Structure is a mandatory, non-negotiable criterion. If an FVG forms but price has not broken past a previously established high (for bullish) or low (for bearish) during or before its creation, the gap should not be traded — regardless of how clean it looks visually. This rule exists to confirm that the move that created the gap was strong enough to shift market structure, not just a temporary spike within an unchanged trend.

# Key Takeaways From Day 17
 
- A Fair Value Gap is a three-candle imbalance where the first and third candles' wicks don't overlap, created by a rapid, one-sided price move.
- The market naturally seeks to fill these imbalances, making FVGs act as "magnets" for future price action.
- An FVG is drawn from the top wick of the candle before the move to the lower wick of the candle after the move.
- Six factors determine a valid, "ultimate" FVG: Unmitigated status, Proper Candle Reaction (body close), Confluence with S/R, Positional Priority, Gann Box filtering, and Break of Structure.
- For bullish trends, the lowest FVG has the highest priority. For bearish trends, the highest FVG has the highest priority.
- The Gann Box tool (0, 0.5, 1 levels) splits a move in half — only trade gaps in the lower half for bullish setups, upper half for bearish setups.
- A wick piercing through the gap is acceptable; a candle body closing completely outside the gap invalidates the setup.
- An FVG without a Break of Structure should never be traded — BOS is a mandatory validation criterion, not optional confluence.
- Trade execution: stop loss at the low of the gap-generating candle, take profit at the high of the overall move.
