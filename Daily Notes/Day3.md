#Topic 1: Candlestick Patterns: 
Candlestick patterns are visual representations of price action that help traders 
understand the battle between buyers and sellers during a specific time frame. Each 
candle shows four important values: open, close, high, and low price. Candlestick 
analysis is mainly used to identify reversals, trend continuation, and market sentiment. 
Green candles generally indicate buyer strength, while red candles represent seller 
dominance. Large candles indicate strong momentum, whereas long wicks show 
rejection of a particular price level. 
Bullish Reversal Patterns 
Bullish reversal patterns suggest that buyers are gaining control and the market may 
move upward. 
Hammer 
A hammer has a small body with a long lower wick. It shows that sellers initially 
pushed the price down, but buyers regained strength and pushed the price back upward 
before the candle closed. This pattern becomes stronger near support levels. 
Bullish Engulfing 
This is a two-candle pattern where a large green candle completely engulfs the 
previous red candle. It indicates strong buying pressure and a possible bullish reversal. 
Tweezer Bottom 
This pattern consists of a red candle followed by a green candle with similar lower 
wicks. It shows that the support area is being respected and buyers are defending the 
price level. 
 
Dragonfly Doji 
This pattern has a long lower wick with opening and closing prices near the same 
level. It represents rejection of lower prices and possible buyer strength. 
 
Bearish Reversal Patterns 
Bearish reversal patterns indicate that sellers are taking control and the market may 
move downward. 
Shooting Star 
A shooting star has a small body with a long upper wick. It shows that buyers pushed 
the price upward, but sellers forced the price back down before closing. 
Bearish Engulfing 
This pattern occurs when a large red candle completely engulfs the previous green 
candle, indicating strong seller dominance. 
Tweezer Top 
A green candle followed by a red candle with matching upper wicks forms a tweezer 
top. This indicates strong resistance and rejection of higher prices. 
 
Momentum and Continuation Patterns 
Momentum Candle 
A momentum candle is significantly larger than previous candles and usually signals 
strong buying or selling activity. In sideways markets, momentum candles often begin 
new trends. 
Marubozu Candle 
A Marubozu candle has a large body with no wicks, showing complete control by 
buyers or sellers throughout the session. 
Multiple Candle Confirmation 
When multiple candles repeatedly reject the same area using their wicks, it strengthens 
the reliability of that support or resistance zone. 
   
 
#Topic 2: Support and Resistance 
Support and resistance are important concepts in technical analysis that represent key 
price levels where market behavior changes. 
- Support acts like a floor where buying pressure becomes strong enough to stop 
price from falling further. 
- Resistance acts like a ceiling where selling pressure becomes strong enough to 
stop price from moving higher. 
These levels are important because traders often react emotionally around these zones, 
making them psychological areas in the market. 
 
Role Reversal Principle 
One of the most important concepts learned was role reversal. 
- Broken support often becomes new resistance. 
- Broken resistance often becomes new support. 
This happens because traders who were trapped in losing positions try to exit when 
price returns to their entry level, creating new buying or selling pressure. 
 
Drawing Support and Resistance 
Support and resistance should be treated as zones rather than exact lines. 
In Range Markets 
The major highs and lows of the range are used to identify key support and resistance 
zones. The “Most Touches Rule” states that levels become stronger when price reacts 
multiple times from the same area. 
In Trending Markets 
Recent swing highs and swing lows are used to identify possible pullback areas and 
trend continuation zones. 
Minor levels should be avoided because too many levels can create confusion and poor 
trade decisions. 
#Topic 3: Analyse Market Trend Like a Pro – The 21 EMA Strategy            
(Deepvue tutorial) 
The 21 EMA (Exponential Moving Average) Strategy is used to identify market trends 
and manage trading risk. The strategy helps traders determine whether market 
conditions are favorable or weak. 
The core idea of the strategy is: 
- Be aggressive during strong trends. 
- Be defensive during weak market conditions. 
The 21 EMA reacts faster to price movement than a simple moving average, making it 
useful for identifying trend direction and momentum. 
 
Aggressive and Defensive Phases 
Aggressive Phase: 
When price remains above a rising 21 EMA, it indicates a healthy uptrend and strong 
momentum. During this phase, traders can take more swing trading opportunities with 
confidence. 
Defensive Phase: 
When price moves below a declining 21 EMA, it signals weak market conditions. 
During this phase, traders should reduce risk, avoid unnecessary trades, and maintain a 
larger cash position. 
This approach helps protect capital during market corrections. 
 
Trend Identification 
A new uptrend is identified when: 
The declining 21 EMA starts flattening. 
Price breaks above the EMA. 
Momentum increases. 
This sequence often marks the beginning of a strong trending move.
