# Topics Learned Today

## Topic 1: Liquidity Sweeps

Today I learned about Liquidity Sweeps, also known as Liquidity Grabs or Liquidity Hunts. Liquidity refers to pending orders in the market, including stop-loss orders, buy limits, and sell limits waiting to be executed.

Large institutions require significant liquidity to execute their massive orders. Since normal market liquidity is often insufficient, institutions frequently create situations that attract retail traders into the market.

This process is known as a liquidity sweep.

Institutions often create fake breakouts above resistance or below support levels. Retail traders enter these breakouts believing a new trend has started. Their stop-loss orders accumulate around key levels, creating liquidity pools.

Once sufficient liquidity is available, institutions execute their large positions and the market rapidly reverses, trapping retail traders.

---

## Types of Liquidity Pools

### Swing Liquidity

Liquidity that exists above swing highs and below swing lows.

### Equal Highs (EQH)

Areas where multiple highs form at similar levels.

### Equal Lows (EQL)

Areas where multiple lows form at similar levels.

### Range Liquidity

Liquidity found at the boundaries of consolidation zones.

Institutions frequently target these locations before initiating reversals.

## Topic 2: Combining Liquidity Sweeps with Market Imbalance

I learned that liquidity sweeps become more reliable when combined with market imbalance, also known as Fair Value Gaps (FVGs).

Imbalances occur when institutions create aggressive price movements, leaving behind unfilled orders.

These areas often act as magnets for future price action.

A high-probability setup occurs when:

* A liquidity pool exists.
* An imbalance aligns with the liquidity area.
* Price sweeps liquidity.
* A reversal candlestick confirms rejection.

This combination provides multiple layers of confirmation and improves trade accuracy.

---

## Topic 3: Institutional Fake Breakouts

Institutions create fake breakouts to attract retail participation and generate liquidity.

The process works as follows:

* Institutions push price beyond a key level.
* Retail traders enter the breakout.
* Stop-loss orders accumulate.
* Institutions use these orders as liquidity.
* Price reverses sharply in the opposite direction.

This process allows institutions to fill large positions while trapping retail traders.

I learned that many breakout failures are actually liquidity sweeps engineered by institutions.
