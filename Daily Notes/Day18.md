# Topics Learned Today
 
## Topic 1: Supply and Demand — The Core Mechanics
 
Supply and demand explain the core mechanics of why a market moves at all. A chart will only move up if there is more buying pressure than selling pressure, and it will only move down if selling pressure exceeds buying pressure. This is the most fundamental truth underlying every other SMC concept learned so far.
 
### Identifying Supply and Demand Zones
 
To effectively trade using supply and demand, you must identify areas on a chart where one side is in complete control:
 
- **Area of Supply:** Identified by the beginning of a fast, sharp downward move, indicating that there are drastically more sellers than buyers.
- **Area of Demand:** Marked by the beginning of a sharp upward move, showing that there are drastically more buyers than sellers.
> It is crucial that these zones are identified by **drastic, sharp moves**. Slow movements only indicate a minor imbalance between buyers and sellers, which is not strong enough to create a reliable trading zone.
 
### The "Costco" Mindset and Trading Against the Crowd
 
A key philosophy in mastering supply and demand is understanding **who is on the other side of your trade**. To be a winning trader, you must trade against losing or "bad" retail traders.
 
This is compared directly to Costco's business model: you want to **"buy in bulk" at low prices** (demand areas) and **sell to retail buyers at higher prices** (supply areas). In other words, think and act like the institution, not the crowd.
 
**The Two Essential Questions** to ask when analyzing any chart:
 
1. Where are the major points where there are drastically more buyers and sellers?
2. Where are the bad traders buying and selling?
A bad trader will typically buy when there are more sellers (inside a supply zone) or sell when there are more buyers (inside a demand zone). Recognizing these patterns lets you exploit their mistakes.
 
### Strengthening Zones with Fair Value Gaps
 
To add further confidence to your supply and demand zones, look for **Fair Value Gaps** connected to the sharp move that created the zone. An FVG sitting alongside a sharp supply or demand move provides additional evidence of a genuinely strong imbalance between buyers and sellers — directly connecting back to what was learned on Day 17.
 
### Executing the Trade
 
1. **Wait** for the price to slowly return to your area of demand.
2. **Enter the trade** by buying from a retail trader who is incorrectly selling inside this demand zone.
3. **Set your stop loss** just below the area of demand to protect your capital.
4. **Set your take profit** at the next area of supply — where you will eventually sell your position to a bad trader who is buying at the top.

## Topic 2: Market Momentum
 
Market momentum describes the overall directional trend of a chart, driven by the ongoing battle between buyers and sellers. During a downward trend, buyers might enter and fight back against sellers, causing small upward pullbacks. Despite these minor shifts in control, the overall momentum of the chart remains bearish.
 
A true reversal of momentum does **not** happen slowly. A chart's direction only fully reverses when a large majority of buyers overtakes the sellers (or vice versa), resulting in a fast, sharp move.
 
### The Trap of Momentum Trading
 
Relying purely on perceived momentum is a common mistake made by inexperienced or "bad" retail traders.
 
Often, retail traders see upward momentum building and decide to buy in. By focusing only on this momentum, they miss the larger picture — this momentum is often leading them directly into an area of supply. Blinded by the building momentum, these traders make the crucial mistake of buying in a zone where there are drastically more sellers than buyers.
 
Understanding the mechanics of supply and demand allows you to avoid this trap and instead capitalize on the mistakes of traders who blindly follow market momentum.

## Topic 3: Retail Trading Psychology
 
The psychology of retail trading — referred to as "bad," "losing," or "inconsistently profitable" traders — is deeply tied to a misunderstanding of core market mechanics.
 
### The Major Psychological Blind Spot
 
A major blind spot for retail traders is forgetting that there is **always someone on the other side of the trade**. Whenever a trader buys a position, someone else is selling it — meaning that opposing person believes the chart is going to move in the opposite direction.
 
### Key Flaws in Retail Psychology
 
- **Blindly Following Surface Signals:** Retail traders frequently fall into the trap of buying into an area of supply because their psychology is driven by surface-level signals — seeing momentum building up, or blindly entering a trade simply because an indicator tells them to.
- **Ignoring Supply and Demand:** Because they're distracted by momentum or indicators, retail traders miss the fundamental reality of the chart — ending up buying in a zone where there are drastically more sellers than buyers, or selling when there are more buyers than sellers.
### Exploiting Retail Psychology
 
The golden rule: **in order to be a winning trader, you must sell to a losing trader.**
 
To capitalize on retail psychology, constantly ask: *"Where are the bad traders buying and selling?"* The ultimate goal is to:
 
1. Buy from a retail trader who is incorrectly selling in a demand zone.
2. Ride the upward trend to an area of supply.
3. Sell your position to a retail trader who is incorrectly buying at the top.

## Topic 4: Price Action Zones
 
Price action zones are defined as areas of supply and demand where there is a massive imbalance between buyers and sellers.
 
### Identifying Valid Zones
 
A true zone is marked at the precise point on a chart where a fast, sharp move begins:
 
- **Supply Zones:** Created at the beginning of a sharp downward drop, indicating sellers have taken complete control and drastically outnumber buyers.
- **Demand Zones:** Located at the starting point of a sharp upward rally, showing buyers are heavily overpowering sellers.
### The Importance of Sharp Movements
 
If a chart slowly trends downward or moves sideways, it does **not** create a reliable price action zone — it simply indicates only slightly more sellers than buyers. To map out actionable zones, you must look for areas where one side drastically outnumbers the other.
 
### Strengthening the Zones
 
Look for fair value gaps connected to the sharp moves. Finding an FVG connected to a zone provides further evidence of the heavy imbalance between buyers and sellers in that specific area.
 
### Trading the Zones
 
Once a strong price action zone is established:
 
1. Wait for the price to **slowly** return to this area.
2. When price slowly approaches a demand zone, this is the optimal entry point for a buy trade.
3. Set stop loss just below the demand zone.
4. Set take profit at the next major supply zone.

## Topic 5: Buying Pressure
 
Buying pressure is the fundamental force that causes a chart to move upward. The only way price will rise is if buying pressure in the market exceeds selling pressure.
 
### Minor vs. Massive Buying Pressure
 
| Type | Description |
|---|---|
| **Minor Buying Pressure** | During a strong downtrend, buyers may enter and fight back, causing small upward pullbacks. Overall momentum remains bearish until buyers gain a significant majority. |
| **Massive Buying Pressure (Demand Zones)** | A true reversal happens when a large majority of buyers suddenly overtakes sellers, creating a fast, sharp upward move. The exact starting point of this surge creates an "area of demand." |
 
### Identifying and Using Strong Buying Pressure
 
To be an effective trader, avoid areas where buying pressure is only slightly higher than selling pressure — this usually shows up as slow or sideways price movement. Instead, exclusively look for zones where buying pressure was so extreme it caused a drastic, sharp upward movement.
 
You can further confirm the absolute dominance of buyers in a specific area by looking for fair value gaps connected to the demand zone.
 
Once an area of extreme buying pressure is located, the strategy is to wait for price to slowly return to that zone — then capitalize on flawed retail trading psychology by buying your position from a "bad" trader who is incorrectly selling right in the middle of this area of overwhelming buyer dominance.

# Questions I Had and Answers I Got
 
## Q1. How sharp does a move need to be to count as a valid supply or demand zone?
The source material emphasizes that the move must be drastic and sharp — not gradual. A slow, grinding move only reflects a minor, temporary imbalance, which doesn't create a zone strong enough for institutions to have left a meaningful footprint. There's no exact numerical threshold given, but the visual cue is a fast, aggressive candle or sequence of candles that clearly breaks away from the recent price action — similar to the kind of move that creates a Fair Value Gap, covered on Day 17. If the move looks unremarkable compared to surrounding price action, it likely isn't strong enough to mark as a valid zone.
 
## Q2. Why do retail traders keep buying into momentum even when it leads them into a supply zone?
Based on the retail psychology concepts learned today, this happens because retail traders react to surface-level signals rather than the underlying mechanics of supply and demand. Momentum is visually obvious and feels intuitive to follow — a chart moving up "looks" bullish. However, this surface-level read ignores the deeper question of whether buyers actually still outnumber sellers at the current price, or whether price is approaching a zone where sellers have historically taken control. Because momentum traders aren't asking "where are the bad traders buying and selling," they end up becoming the bad trader themselves — buying right into an area of supply.
 
## Q3. How is Market Momentum different from a genuine trend reversal?
Market momentum reflects the ongoing battle between buyers and sellers, and it can include temporary pullbacks without an actual reversal — for example, minor buying pressure during a downtrend. A genuine reversal only happens when a large majority of one side completely overtakes the other, producing a fast, sharp move. The key distinction is the size and speed of the shift: minor, slow shifts are just noise within the existing trend, while a true reversal is the kind of drastic move that creates an actual supply or demand zone.
 
## Q4. Why is it important to think about "who" is on the other side of my trade, rather than just the price levels?
Focusing purely on price levels treats trading as a mechanical exercise, but the supply and demand framework is built on the idea that markets move because of real participants making decisions — some of whom make consistent mistakes. By identifying where retail traders are likely to be trapped (buying into supply, selling into demand), you're not just finding a price level — you're finding the exact point where a losing trade is about to be opened by someone else. Their eventual capitulation often becomes part of the liquidity that fuels your trade's move, connecting directly to the liquidity concepts learned earlier in the internship.
 
## Q5. Should I always wait for price to return slowly to a zone, or can a fast return still be tradeable?
The source specifically describes waiting for price to "slowly return" to the area of demand before executing the trade. A slow return suggests a controlled retracement where the market is giving you time to confirm the zone is being respected, rather than violently slamming through it. A very fast, aggressive return to the zone could be a sign of strong momentum continuing in the original direction, which might blow through the zone entirely rather than respect it.
