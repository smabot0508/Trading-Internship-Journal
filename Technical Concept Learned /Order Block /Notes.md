# Order Blocks

## Introduction

Order Blocks are one of the core concepts in Smart Money Concepts (SMC). They represent areas where large institutional traders, banks, and smart money participants have entered the market with significant buying or selling pressure.

Unlike traditional support and resistance levels, Order Blocks help traders identify the actual footprint of institutional activity. These zones often become important areas where price may react in the future.

Understanding Order Blocks allows traders to align themselves with institutional order flow rather than following retail market participants.

---

## What is an Order Block?

An Order Block is the final candle or group of candles before a strong impulsive move that creates a significant market reaction.

This area represents the location where smart money accumulated or distributed large positions before causing a major movement in price.

Bullish Order Block:

* Forms before a strong upward move.
* Indicates institutional buying.
* Acts as a potential demand area.

Bearish Order Block:

* Forms before a strong downward move.
* Indicates institutional selling.
* Acts as a potential supply area.

---

## Why Order Blocks Matter

Institutional traders cannot enter massive positions at once without moving the market against themselves.

Because of this, they accumulate positions gradually and often return to these areas later to complete their orders.

As a result, Order Blocks often become areas where price reacts strongly.

Benefits of identifying Order Blocks:

* Helps locate institutional activity.
* Provides high-probability entry zones.
* Improves risk-to-reward opportunities.
* Works well with Market Structure and Liquidity concepts.
* Helps avoid retail trading traps.

---

## The Three Rules of a Valid Order Block

Not every strong price movement creates a valid Order Block.

For an Order Block to be valid, it must satisfy three strict conditions.

### 1. Inefficiency or Imbalance Must Exist

The move must create an imbalance or Fair Value Gap (FVG).

This imbalance shows that price moved so aggressively that not all orders were efficiently matched.

Signs of an imbalance:

* Strong impulsive move.
* Visible gap between candles.
* Fair Value Gap present.

Without an imbalance, the Order Block is considered invalid.

---

### 2. The Order Block Must Be Unmitigated

An Order Block should remain untouched after its creation.

If price revisits the zone and touches it, the Order Block is considered mitigated or tested.

Characteristics of an Unmitigated Order Block:

* Fresh institutional zone.
* Higher probability of reaction.
* Better trading opportunity.

Once price returns and tests the zone, the effectiveness often decreases.

---

### 3. It Must Cause BOS or CHOCH

A valid Order Block must create a structural impact on the market.

This can happen through:

#### Break of Structure (BOS)

A BOS occurs when the market continues its trend.

Example:

* Uptrend creates Higher Highs.
* Price breaks previous highs.
* Trend continuation is confirmed.

#### Change of Character (CHOCH)

A CHOCH occurs when the market changes direction.

Example:

* Uptrend breaks previous Higher Low.
* Lower Low forms.
* Trend reversal begins.

Without BOS or CHOCH, the Order Block is not considered valid.

---

## How to Draw an Order Block

Step 1:
Identify a strong impulsive move.

Step 2:
Locate the first candle before the impulsive move begins.

Step 3:
Draw a rectangle covering the entire candle:

* Top wick
* Candle body
* Bottom wick

Step 4:
Wait for market confirmation through BOS or CHOCH.

Step 5:
Mark the zone and wait for price to revisit it.

## Order Blocks vs Support and Resistance

| Order Block                  | Support & Resistance             |
| ---------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Institutional activity       | Historical price reactions       |
| Created from impulsive moves | Created from multiple rejections |
| Requires imbalance           | No imbalance required            |
| Usually one-time use         | Can be tested multiple times     |
| Linked with BOS and CHOCH    | Linked with price reactions      |

## Smart Money and Order Blocks

Smart Money refers to institutions, hedge funds, banks, and professional traders who move large amounts of capital.

Their activities create:

* Strong impulsive moves.
* Market Structure Shifts.
* Liquidity Sweeps.
* Order Blocks.

Following Order Blocks helps traders align with institutional activity instead of trading against it.

---

## Inducement Traps

Institutions often create inducements to trap retail traders.

How it works:

1. A minor support or resistance level forms.
2. Retail traders enter positions.
3. Stop losses accumulate around the level.
4. Smart money pushes price through the level.
5. Liquidity is collected.
6. Price moves toward the original Order Block.

This process is called an Inducement Trap.

---

## Breaker Blocks

A Breaker Block is a failed Order Block that changes its role.

Example:

* Bullish Order Block gets broken.
* Market creates a CHOCH.
* The broken Bullish Order Block becomes resistance.

Similarly:

* Broken Bearish Order Block can become support.

Breaker Blocks provide additional trading opportunities after market structure shifts.

---

## Multi-Timeframe Confirmation

Professional traders do not enter a trade simply because price touches an Order Block.

Instead they use Multi-Timeframe Confirmation.

### Higher Timeframe

Identify a valid Order Block on:

* Daily Chart
* 4-Hour Chart
* Weekly Chart

### Lower Timeframe

Wait for confirmation on:

* 1 Hour
* 15 Minutes
* 5 Minutes

Look for:

* CHOCH
* BOS
* Engulfing Candles
* Rejection Candles
* Momentum Shifts

Only then enter the trade.

## Common Mistakes

### Mistake 1

Assuming every sharp move creates an Order Block.

### Mistake 2

Ignoring the imbalance requirement.

### Mistake 3

Drawing Order Blocks before BOS or CHOCH confirmation.

### Mistake 4

Trading mitigated Order Blocks.

### Mistake 5

Treating Order Blocks exactly like Support and Resistance.

---

## Risk Management

For Order Block trading:

* Enter after confirmation.
* Place Stop Loss outside the Order Block.
* Target the next liquidity area.
* Maintain at least a 2:1 Risk-to-Reward ratio.

Risk management is more important than finding perfect entries.

## Key Takeaways

* Order Blocks represent institutional trading activity.
* A valid Order Block requires imbalance, unmitigated status, and BOS or CHOCH.
* Smart money uses inducement traps to collect liquidity.
* Order Blocks work best when combined with Market Structure.
* Multi-Timeframe Confirmation increases trade quality.
* Breaker Blocks form when Order Blocks fail.
* Proper risk management is essential for long-term success.

Order Blocks provide traders with a structured method of identifying institutional footprints in the market.
