# Fair Value Gap (FVG)

## Introduction

Fair Value Gap (FVG) is one of the core concepts in Smart Money Concepts (SMC). It represents an imbalance in the market that is created when price moves aggressively in one direction, leaving behind an area where little or no trading activity occurred.

These imbalances often attract future price action because markets naturally seek efficiency and balance. Traders use FVGs to identify potential entry zones, continuation opportunities, and areas where institutional activity may have taken place.

---

## What is a Fair Value Gap?

A Fair Value Gap is a three-candle price pattern that forms when the market moves so quickly that the wicks of the first and third candles fail to overlap.

This creates a "gap" or imbalance between buyers and sellers.

In simple terms:

* Strong buying creates a Bullish FVG.
* Strong selling creates a Bearish FVG.
* Price often revisits these zones before continuing the trend.

---

## How an FVG is Formed

### Bullish Fair Value Gap

1. Price is moving normally.
2. A large bullish impulse candle appears.
3. Price moves upward aggressively.
4. The first candle's upper wick and the third candle's lower wick do not overlap.
5. The area between them becomes a Bullish FVG.

### Bearish Fair Value Gap

1. Price is moving normally.
2. A large bearish impulse candle appears.
3. Price drops aggressively.
4. The surrounding candles fail to overlap.
5. The area becomes a Bearish FVG.

---

## Market Imbalance

A market imbalance occurs when buyers or sellers dominate the market and force price to move rapidly in one direction.

Because the move happens so quickly:

* Opposing participants cannot react.
* Orders remain unfilled.
* Price leaves behind an inefficient area.

This inefficiency is what creates the Fair Value Gap.

---

## Why Price Returns to FVGs

Markets naturally seek equilibrium.

When price moves too fast:

* Unfilled orders remain.
* Institutions may need additional liquidity.
* Market participants seek fair value.

As a result, price frequently retraces back into the Fair Value Gap before continuing its original move.

This retracement often provides trading opportunities.

---

## Break of Structure (BOS) and FVG

A Break of Structure (BOS) significantly strengthens an FVG.

### Bullish BOS

* Price breaks a previous high.
* Strong buying enters the market.
* A bullish FVG forms.

### Bearish BOS

* Price breaks a previous low.
* Strong selling enters the market.
* A bearish FVG forms.

An FVG formed after a BOS generally has a higher probability of success because market momentum has already been confirmed.

---

## Support and Resistance Confluence

FVGs become stronger when they align with:

* Previous support levels
* Previous resistance levels
* Supply zones
* Demand zones

Multiple confirmations increase the probability that the market will react from the gap.

---

## Unmitigated vs Mitigated FVG

### Unmitigated FVG

* Price has never returned to the gap.
* Considered fresh.
* Higher probability setup.

### Mitigated FVG

* Price has already tested the gap.
* Some orders may have been filled.
* Usually weaker than a fresh FVG.

Traders often prioritize unmitigated Fair Value Gaps.

---

## Key Characteristics of a Valid FVG

A high-quality Fair Value Gap should satisfy several conditions:

1. The gap should be unmitigated.
2. Price should react properly when revisiting the zone.
3. Support or resistance should align with the gap.
4. The gap should occur after a Break of Structure.
5. The gap should be positioned in a strong area of the trend.
6. The market should show clear directional momentum.

---

## Advantages of FVGs

* Provides precise entry zones.
* Helps identify institutional activity.
* Works across multiple timeframes.
* Can be combined with BOS and Liquidity concepts.
* Improves risk-to-reward opportunities.

---

## Key Takeaways

* Fair Value Gaps represent market imbalances.
* They are formed by strong impulsive price movements.
* Price often returns to fill these gaps before continuing the trend.
* Break of Structure improves the reliability of an FVG.
* Unmitigated FVGs are generally stronger than mitigated ones.
* FVGs should always be combined with market structure and other confirmations.

---

## Conclusion

Fair Value Gaps help traders identify areas where price moved inefficiently due to aggressive buying or selling. By combining FVGs with Market Structure, Break of Structure, Liquidity, and Support and Resistance, traders can identify higher-probability trading opportunities and improve their understanding of institutional market behavior.
